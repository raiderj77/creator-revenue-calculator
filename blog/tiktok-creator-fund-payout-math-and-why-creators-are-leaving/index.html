TikTok Creator Fund payout math and why creators are leaving

Published May 29, 2026 · Jason Ramirez

Revenue Disclaimer: Revenue estimates are approximations based on publicly available data. Actual earnings may vary significantly.

TikTok Creator Fund Payouts: The Math That Explains Why Creators Are Leaving

The short answer: The TikTok Creator Fund pays most creators between $0.02 and $0.04 per 1,000 views. That works out to $20-$40 per million views. That rate has dropped over time. The fund has a fixed pool of money, and it gets split among more and more creators. For comparison, YouTube AdSense typically pays $1,000-$3,000 per million views in the US. The gap is not close.

What does TikTok actually pay per 1,000 views?

TikTok does not publish a fixed rate. The Creator Fund works like this: there is a fixed pool of money each day. TikTok splits that pool among all eligible creators. Your share depends on your views, engagement, and content scores. When more creators join, each person gets a smaller piece.

Independent creator reports collected by Influencer Marketing Hub put the range at $0.02-$0.04 per 1,000 views. Some creators have reported rates as low as $0.01 per 1,000 views during busy periods. TikTok's own Creator Fund FAQ says the fund is "based on a number of factors." It does not name a minimum or maximum rate.

Here is the math for a creator who gets 10 million views in one month:

  • At $0.03 per 1,000 views: $300
  • At $0.04 per 1,000 views: $400
  • At $0.02 per 1,000 views: $200

Ten million views in a month is not easy to achieve. Most full-time creators on other platforms would earn much more from that same audience.

How does TikTok's payout compare to YouTube?

YouTube pays much more per view than TikTok's Creator Fund. YouTube's Partner Program gives creators 55 percent of the ad revenue their videos earn. US CPMs on YouTube typically range from $2 to $10. TikTok's Creator Fund pays $0.02 to $0.04 per 1,000 views by comparison.

The reason YouTube pays more is simple. YouTube shares ad revenue with creators. TikTok's Creator Fund is a fixed pool that gets thinner as more creators join.

YouTube's Partner Program overview confirms creators get 55% of ad revenue from their content. US YouTube CPMs reported by creators typically range from $2-$10 per 1,000 views. That depends on the topic. Finance and business content often goes above $10. Statista data on YouTube CPM rates shows average CPMs across categories sitting in the $4-$6 range for US audiences.

So a creator with 10 million monthly views on YouTube might earn:

  • At $3 CPM (low, broad entertainment niche): $30,000
  • At $1.50 CPM (after YouTube's 45% cut, conservative): $15,000
  • TikTok Creator Fund for the same views: $200-$400

The ratio is roughly 40 to 1 or even 100 to 1 in YouTube's favor. It depends on the niche and where the audience lives.

Why did TikTok's per-view rate drop over time?

The fund launched in 2020 with $200 million. TikTok later expanded it to $1 billion over three years. The problem is built into how the fund works. When TikTok started the fund, far fewer creators qualified. As millions of creators joined and total views grew, the same fixed pool got split into smaller and smaller pieces.

Creator Hank Green documented this publicly in late 2021. He noted that his per-view rate had dropped a lot since the fund launched. His point: the fund looked generous at the start because few creators qualified. The math was always going to get worse as TikTok grew.

TikTok has since launched the TikTok Creativity Program Beta, now called the Creator Rewards Program in some markets. It requires videos to be at least one minute long. It aims to pay higher rates for longer content. Early reports show rates of $0.40-$1.00 per 1,000 views for qualifying content. That is better, but still well below YouTube's ad-share model for most niches.

What are creators doing instead of relying on the Creator Fund?

Most creators are keeping their TikTok audience but finding income elsewhere. They are using brand sponsorships, Patreon memberships, or other platforms. A creator with 500,000 followers can earn $500 to $2,500 per sponsored post, according to Creator IQ benchmarks. That one deal can already beat what the Fund pays on millions of organic views.

Creators leaving the fund are not leaving TikTok. They are just stopping to rely on the fund for income. They are building money streams in other places.

Here are the main options:

Brand sponsorships: A creator with 500,000 followers and strong engagement can charge $500-$2,500 per sponsored post, according to industry rate benchmarks from Creator IQ. One deal like that can beat months of Creator Fund income.

Driving traffic to owned platforms: Many TikTok creators use the platform to grow a Substack newsletter or Patreon membership. Patreon's own creator data shows that creators with 1,000 patrons at $5 a month earn $5,000 monthly before Patreon's 5-12% platform fee. That income repeats every month and does not depend on a fixed pool being split up.

YouTube Shorts and long-form: Creators who expand TikTok content for YouTube can access the ad-share model. YouTube Shorts has its own revenue pool similar to TikTok's structure, but long-form YouTube content earns through standard AdSense.

Should you use the TikTok Creator Fund at all?

If you qualify, there is no reason not to sign up. Free money is free money, even at $0.03 per 1,000 views. The mistake is treating it as your main income or using it to judge whether your content is doing well.

The fund cannot pay creators at the same rates as ad-revenue-share models. TikTok built a fixed pool, not a percentage-of-revenue model. That choice gets more costly as the platform grows.

Build on TikTok if your audience is there. Then move that audience to platforms with better economics. Creators who are frustrated with TikTok payouts usually never built that second layer.

The math was always in the fine print. Now you have read it.

Frequently asked questions

How much does the TikTok Creator Fund actually pay per 1,000 views?

The TikTok Creator Fund pays roughly $0.02-$0.04 per 1,000 views. A video with 1 million views earns only $20-$40. Compare that to YouTube's average CPM of $3-$10 per 1,000 views, where the same traffic could earn $300-$1,000 or more. The Creator Fund's rate also drops as more creators join, since the fixed pool gets divided among more people.

Why are TikTok creators switching to YouTube or Substack?

Creators are leaving because YouTube, Substack, and Patreon pay much more per audience member. YouTube's ad revenue share, Substack subscriptions, and Patreon memberships reward engaged audiences, not just raw view counts. A TikTok creator with 500,000 followers might earn less than $50 a month from the Creator Fund. That same audience on Substack, converting at just 1% to a $5 a month subscription, generates $250 a month.

What replaced the TikTok Creator Fund?

TikTok replaced the Creator Fund with the Creativity Program Beta in 2023. It targets longer videos, over one minute, and reportedly pays 3-5x more than the original fund. But payouts still vary a lot and are not fully explained. Many creators report inconsistent earnings. They keep spreading their income across sponsorships, merchandise, and off-platform subscriptions instead of relying only on TikTok's built-in monetization.

How do sponsorships compare to platform payouts for TikTok creators?

Sponsorships typically pay 10-50x more than Creator Fund payouts for the same content. A mid-tier TikTok creator with 200,000 followers can charge $500-$2,000 per sponsored post. The same creator earns roughly $4-$8 from the Creator Fund on a 200,000-view video. That math is why creators treat platform funds as small side money while making sponsorships their main income source.

Is the TikTok Creator Fund worth it for small creators?

For most small creators, the Creator Fund is not worth building a strategy around. A creator averaging 50,000 views per video earns about $1-$2 per post from the fund. The real value of a TikTok audience is using it to send people to higher-paying platforms like Patreon or an email list. It can also help attract brand deals, where even smaller creators with strong engagement can earn real sponsorship money.
