How much does patreon take?

Published June 19, 2026 · Jason Ramirez

Revenue Disclaimer: Revenue estimates are approximations based on publicly available data. Actual earnings may vary significantly.

How Much Does Patreon Take? The Real Fee Breakdown for Creators

The short answer: Patreon takes between 5% and 12% of your monthly earnings depending on your plan. On top of that, you pay payment processing fees. Those are roughly 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction for larger payments, and up to 5% + $0.10 for smaller ones. On a $1,000 month, you realistically keep somewhere between $850 and $880 after all fees, not the full thousand.

What are Patreon's actual platform fee tiers?

Patreon charges a percentage of your earnings based on which plan you pick. The three plans are Free (Patreon takes 8%), Pro (Patreon takes 8%), and Premium (Patreon takes 12%). There is no longer a "Lite" plan.

According to Patreon's own pricing page, the current structure as of 2024 is:

  • Free plan: 8% of monthly revenue
  • Pro plan: 8% of monthly revenue (adds membership tiers, analytics, and app integrations)
  • Premium plan: 12% of monthly revenue (adds a dedicated partner manager and team accounts)

Most working creators use Pro. The jump to Premium only makes sense if you earn enough that the extra support is worth 4 more percentage points. On $5,000 per month, that 4% difference is $200. That is real money.

What are the payment processing fees on top of that?

Patreon charges a standard rate of 2.9% plus $0.30 per transaction for most payments. For smaller payments, the rate is 5% + $0.10. These fees add to your overall costs.

Payment processing is a separate fee. Patreon passes it through from its payment partners. It is not included in the platform percentage.

Patreon's fee documentation shows two processing rate structures:

  • Standard rate: 2.9% + $0.30 per successful charge (for most payments)
  • Micropayment rate: 5% + $0.10 for payments under a certain threshold (roughly under $3)

This matters a lot if you have a $1 or $2 tier. A patron paying $1 per month triggers the micropayment rate. That costs you $0.05 + $0.10, which equals $0.15 in processing alone. That is 15% of the payment gone before Patreon's 8% platform cut. You net roughly $0.77 on a $1 pledge.

There are also currency conversion fees, typically 2.5%, when patrons pay in currencies other than USD.

What does Patreon actually cost on a real monthly income example?

For a creator with 200 patrons at $5/month each, total gross is $1,000. After an 8% platform fee and payment processing fees of about $89, the net payout is $831. That works out to roughly 16.9% in total fees.

Here is the math for a creator on the Pro plan with 200 patrons paying $5/month each. Total gross: $1,000.

Fee Type Rate Amount
Patreon platform fee (Pro) 8% $80.00
Payment processing (2.9% + $0.30 x 200) varies $29 + $60 = $89.00
Total fees $169.00
Creator net payout $831.00

That is an effective total fee rate of about 16.9% on $1,000 gross. The platform percentage is only half the story. Processing fees hit harder at lower pledge amounts because of the fixed $0.30 per transaction.

If those same 200 patrons each paid $10 instead ($2,000 gross):

  • Platform fee: $160
  • Processing: $58 + $60 = $118
  • Net: $1,722 (effective rate: ~13.9%)

The effective rate drops as the average pledge size goes up. This is why many experienced creators consolidate tiers instead of offering $1 options.

How does Patreon's cut compare to other creator platforms?

Patreon is not the cheapest option, but it is not the most expensive either.

  • Substack takes 10% of paid subscription revenue, with Stripe processing fees (~2.9% + $0.30) on top. Substack's pricing page confirms this flat rate with no tiered plans.
  • YouTube memberships take 30% off the top, per YouTube's channel membership documentation.
  • Ko-fi charges 0% platform fee on its free plan (processing fees still apply), though it lacks Patreon's membership infrastructure.
  • Memberful charges 4.9% on its Pro plan with no monthly fee. That makes it cheaper than Patreon for high-volume creators who already have their own audience.

For a creator doing $3,000/month, the difference between Patreon Pro (8%) and Memberful (4.9%) is $93/month, or roughly $1,116/year. That is not nothing.

Does Patreon charge anything else I might miss?

There are a few less-obvious costs worth knowing.

Payout fees: Withdrawing your balance to a US bank account via standard ACH is free. Withdrawing via PayPal costs extra. PayPal charges on their end, typically 2% capped at $20 for domestic transfers. Wire transfers cost $20 per withdrawal.

Declined payment handling: Patreon retries failed payments and does not charge you for those retries. But you also do not earn on declined pledges. Patron churn from failed cards is a real revenue leak. It does not show up in fee calculations, but it does affect your actual take-home.

Taxes: Patreon does not withhold income tax. If you are in the US and earn over $400 net from self-employment, you owe self-employment tax. That is 15.3% on net earnings up to the Social Security wage base for 2024, per IRS Publication 334. Factor this into your real net, not just platform fees.

What is the actual bottom line for creators?

Plan on keeping roughly 83 to 87 cents of every dollar your patrons pledge. That assumes US-based patrons, the Pro plan, and average pledge sizes above $5. Lower pledge amounts and international patrons will push that number down. Higher average pledges push it up slightly.

The math is not devastating, but it is not the "keep almost everything" pitch you sometimes see. Know your numbers before you set your tier prices.

Frequently asked questions

How much does Patreon take from creator earnings?

Patreon takes between 5% and 12% of your monthly earnings, depending on your plan. The Lite plan charges 5%, Pro takes 8%, and Premium takes 12%. These fees are deducted before payouts. Payment processing fees are added on top, typically 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction for larger payments. For a creator earning $1,000/month on the Pro plan, expect roughly $80 in platform fees plus additional payment processing costs.

What is the difference between Patreon's Lite, Pro, and Premium plans?

The three Patreon plans differ in features and fees. Lite (5%) is basic membership with limited tools. Pro (8%) unlocks analytics, membership tiers, and app integrations. It is the most popular choice for most creators. Premium (12%) adds dedicated partner support and merchandise features. It works best for larger operations. Most mid-sized creators find Pro offers the best balance between cost and features, though the higher fee adds up as your audience grows.

How do Patreon fees compare to Substack fees?

Substack charges a flat 10% of subscription revenue. Patreon ranges from 5% to 12% depending on your plan. For a newsletter creator, Substack's simplicity can be appealing. But a Patreon Pro creator earning the same amount pays only 8%. Both platforms add payment processing fees separately. Creators running communities or multimedia content often prefer Patreon's flexibility. Writers focused purely on newsletters often find Substack's all-in-one setup worth the slightly higher cut.

Does Patreon charge payment processing fees on top of its platform fee?

Yes, Patreon charges payment processing fees separately from its platform percentage. For most transactions, this is approximately 2.9% + $0.30 for payments over $3. Smaller pledges use a higher micropayment rate of around 5% + $0.10. These fees are paid by the creator, not the patron. On a $5 pledge, processing fees can take a large share. That makes lower-tier memberships less profitable than they first appear on a revenue calculator.

How much does a Patreon creator actually take home after all fees?

After Patreon's platform fee and payment processing costs, most creators take home roughly 80 to 88 cents per dollar earned. On the Pro plan with standard processing, a creator earning $2,000/month might net closer to $1,650-$1,720 after all deductions. Patreon does not withhold income taxes, so self-employed creators should set aside an additional 25 to 30% for taxes.
