Sponsorship rates: what audience size unlocks which deal tier

Published May 17, 2026 · Jason Ramirez

Revenue Disclaimer: Revenue estimates are approximations based on publicly available data. Actual earnings may vary significantly.

Sponsorship Rates: What Audience Size Unlocks Which Deal Tier

The short answer: Most brand deals follow a loose tier system based on subscriber or follower count. But raw numbers matter less than niche, engagement rate, and audience demographics. A 10,000-subscriber finance channel can command higher CPMs than a 100,000-subscriber general lifestyle channel. Below are the actual rate ranges, the math behind them, and what brands are really buying.

Does follower count actually determine your rate?

Follower count sets the floor of the conversation, not the ceiling. Brands use it as a quick filter. They want to know if it is worth starting a conversation at all. But the final number on a contract depends on engagement rate, audience income level, and how closely your content matches the product.

The industry shorthand for this is CPM-on-deliverables. That means brands think about what they are paying per thousand views a sponsored segment will realistically get. They are not thinking about per thousand followers you have. A creator with 50,000 subscribers but a 15% engagement rate on YouTube may deliver more real eyeballs on a sponsored segment than a creator with 200,000 subscribers and a 2% engagement rate. That math changes the whole negotiation.

Influencer Marketing Hub's 2024 Benchmark Report found that average engagement rates drop sharply as follower counts rise. Micro-influencers with 10,000 to 100,000 followers averaged around 3.86% engagement on Instagram. Accounts above 1 million averaged just 1.21%. Brands that pay attention to that data are more and more willing to pay micro-creators more per follower than they pay mega-creators.

What are the actual rate ranges by audience size tier?

Rates scale from roughly $50 per video at the nano level to $50,000 or more at the mega tier. Nano creators with 1,000 to 10,000 followers typically earn $50 to $300 per integration. Mid-tier channels with around 100,000 to 500,000 subscribers commonly see $2,000 to $10,000, based on widely reported creator disclosures.

Here are working ranges based on reported industry data and creator disclosures, not aspirational headlines.

Nano (1,000-10,000 subscribers/followers)

Most nano creators are not getting inbound brand deals yet. When they do land one, rates for a dedicated YouTube integration typically fall between $50 and $300 per video. Flat-fee gifting deals, meaning product only with no cash, are common at this tier. Later's creator rate data puts Instagram story rates for nano accounts at roughly $10-$100 per story.

Micro (10,000-100,000 subscribers/followers)

This is where cash deals become reliable. YouTube mid-roll or end-card integrations typically run $200-$1,500 per placement. Dedicated videos, where the entire video is the sponsorship, run $500-$5,000 depending on niche. Finance, software, and B2B niches sit at the high end. Lifestyle and general entertainment sit at the low end. Podcast sponsorships at this tier generally follow a CPM of $20-$40 per thousand downloads per episode, per Spotify's 2023 Podcast Advertising Playbook.

Mid-tier (100,000-500,000 subscribers/followers)

YouTube integrations, meaning 60 to 90 second sponsor reads within a video, typically run $1,500-$8,000 per placement. Dedicated sponsorship videos run $3,000-$20,000. Podcast CPMs at this tier often negotiate up to $40-$60 per thousand downloads. The audience is large enough to show consistent results. Newsletter sponsorships follow similar CPM logic. Paved's marketplace data shows newsletters with 50,000 to 200,000 subscribers averaging $30-$60 CPM for a primary placement.

Macro (500,000-2,000,000 subscribers/followers)

Integration rates typically range from $8,000-$30,000 per video. Dedicated videos can reach $50,000-$100,000 for the right niche. At this tier, brands often want exclusivity clauses. That means no competitor mentions for 30 to 90 days. Those clauses should add 15 to 25% to your quoted rate. Multi-video package deals become common. Brands may also ask for usage rights to reuse your content in their own ads. That is a separate fee category.

Mega (2,000,000+ subscribers/followers)

Rates are mostly negotiated case by case. There are no reliable public benchmarks at this level. Single integrations at this tier can exceed $100,000. Flat-fee brand partnerships, set up quarterly or annually, replace per-video deals for many creators at this scale.

How do you calculate what to charge?

Many mid-tier creators use a simple starting formula. Take your average view count per video over the last 90 days. Divide by 1,000. Then multiply by your target CPM rate.

Example: 80,000 average views divided by 1,000 equals 80. At a $25 CPM for a general audience, that is $2,000 per integration. At a $50 CPM for a personal finance audience, that is $4,000.

YouTube's own Help Center does not publish sponsorship rate guidance. That is between you and the brand. But it does confirm that AdSense CPMs vary widely by niche. That is a useful signal for what brands in your category already pay for attention.

The CPM you can charge goes up with how much the brand earns per customer. A software company with a $500 annual subscription can justify a $75 CPM. A consumer goods company with a $30 product margin cannot.

What kills a deal even when your numbers look right?

Audience geography is the most common deal-killer. Brands rarely explain this directly. A channel with 90% of viewers outside the US, UK, Canada, and Australia will face steep rate cuts. Most brand budgets are geo-targeted. Statista's digital advertising spend data consistently shows North America accounting for over 40% of global digital ad spend. That explains why brands pay a premium for US-heavy audiences.

Three things consistently derail sponsorship conversations no matter the audience size.

First, audience geography. A channel with 90% of viewers outside the US, UK, Canada, and Australia will face steep rate reductions. Most brand budgets are geo-targeted. Statista's digital advertising spend data consistently shows North America accounting for over 40% of global digital ad spend. That is why brands pay a premium for US-heavy audiences.

Second, inconsistent posting. Brands buying a placement in March want to know your April video will also perform well. Erratic upload schedules make your average view count an unreliable predictor. Brands know this.

Third, audience-product mismatch. A brand selling project management software does not want to pay finance-niche CPMs to reach an audience that came for stock tips. Niche alignment is not a soft factor. It directly affects conversion rates. That affects whether the brand comes back for a second deal.

Should you use a sponsorship marketplace or go direct?

Marketplaces like Grapevine, Creator.co, and Passionfroot are useful for nano and micro creators who need inbound deal flow. The tradeoff is that marketplace deals typically pay 20 to 40% less than direct outreach. The platform takes a cut, and brands using marketplaces are often running lower-budget campaigns.

Direct outreach to brand marketing teams or their agencies becomes worth the effort once you are consistently above 50,000 subscribers. You should also be able to show clean analytics screenshots. A one-page media kit with your average views, audience demographics from YouTube Studio, and two or three past brand references is enough to open most conversations.

The math on going direct is simple. If a marketplace deal pays $1,500 and a comparable direct deal pays $2,200, the 10 emails it takes to land that direct deal are worth sending.

Frequently asked questions

What audience size do you need to land your first paid sponsorship?

Most creators land their first paid sponsorship around 1,000 to 5,000 engaged followers or subscribers. Brands care more about niche relevance and engagement rate than raw numbers at this stage. A 2,000-subscriber YouTube channel covering personal finance will attract fintech sponsors faster than a 20,000-subscriber general lifestyle channel. Micro-sponsorship platforms like Passionfroot and Creator.co connect small creators with brands willing to pay $50-$500 per placement.

How much do sponsors typically pay per 1,000 views or subscribers?

Sponsorship rates generally range from $20 to $50 per 1,000 views (CPM) on YouTube. Niche heavily influences that number. Business, finance, and SaaS-adjacent content can command $50-$100+ CPM. Entertainment sits closer to $15-$25. On newsletters, expect $30-$50 per 1,000 subscribers for a dedicated send. These figures are negotiated flat fees, not programmatic ad revenue. Your pitch, audience demographics, and conversion proof directly affect the final rate.

What sponsorship deal tiers exist and what unlocks each one?

There are roughly three tiers. Micro deals run $100-$1,000 per deal for audiences under 10,000. Mid-tier deals run $1,000-$10,000 for audiences of 10,000 to 100,000. Premium deals start at $10,000 and up for audiences above 100,000 with strong engagement. Moving between tiers depends on audience size, engagement rate, niche CPM value, and media kit quality. Creators with smaller but highly targeted audiences, like a Substack covering B2B SaaS, routinely skip tiers. Advertiser ROI justifies premium pricing no matter the subscriber count.

Do Substack or Patreon earnings count when negotiating sponsorship rates?

Yes. Substack and Patreon revenue signals audience loyalty and willingness to pay. That strengthens your sponsorship negotiation. A creator with 500 paying Patreon members shows proven conversion ability. Sponsors value that more than passive follower counts. Including paid subscriber numbers or monthly recurring revenue in your media kit helps. It positions you as someone with a high-intent audience. Even modest figures like $500 MRR can justify higher flat-fee rates than your raw free-subscriber number alone would suggest.

Is sponsorship income realistic as a primary revenue stream for small creators?

Sponsorship can be a meaningful income stream. But it rarely replaces a salary at under 50,000 followers without strong niche positioning. A realistic mid-tier creator earning $3,000-$5,000 per month from sponsorships typically holds 30,000 to 80,000 engaged subscribers in a monetizable niche and closes two to four deals monthly. Most small creators treat sponsorships as extra income alongside Patreon, digital products, or affiliate revenue. Diversifying reduces the feast-or-famine cycle that makes sponsorship-only income unstable at early audience sizes.
