# YouTube RPM vs CPM: Which Metric Actually Predicts Your Payout

**The short answer:** RPM predicts your payout. CPM measures what advertisers pay per thousand ad impressions. RPM measures what you actually earn per thousand total video views, after YouTube takes its 45% cut and after counting views that never showed an ad. If you want to know what lands in your bank account, watch RPM.

---

## What is CPM, and why does YouTube show it to you?

CPM (cost per mille) is the price advertisers bid and pay for one thousand ad impressions on your content. It is a number meant for advertisers. YouTube shows it in your analytics because it tells you how valuable your audience is to buyers. It does not tell you how much money you made.

According to [YouTube's own Help Center](https://support.google.com/youtube/answer/9314964), CPM reflects the cost advertisers pay before YouTube's revenue share is applied. That revenue share is not a secret. YouTube keeps 45% of ad revenue and passes 55% to creators, as stated in [YouTube's partner program overview](https://support.google.com/youtube/answer/72857). So even before you count unmonetized views, your share of any CPM figure is already cut nearly in half.

A channel with a $10 CPM does not earn $10 per thousand views. It earns $5.50 per thousand ad impressions. That is a different number entirely.

---

## What is RPM, and how is it calculated?

RPM (revenue per mille) is the number that maps to your bank account. It is calculated by dividing your total estimated revenue by your total video views, then multiplying by 1,000. That total revenue includes AdSense, channel memberships, Super Chats, and YouTube Premium revenue. All of that gets divided across every view, even views where no ad ran.

The formula YouTube uses, [documented in their Help Center](https://support.google.com/youtube/answer/9314964), is:

```
RPM = (Total Revenue / Total Views) x 1,000
```

Because RPM uses all views in the denominator, it will always be lower than CPM. That gap is not a mistake. It reflects two real things. First, YouTube keeps 45% of ad revenue. Second, many of your views will never trigger a monetizable ad impression. Viewers using ad blockers, viewers who skip before the ad counts, and viewers in low-fill-rate regions all add views without adding revenue.

---

## How big is the gap between CPM and RPM in practice?

The gap is significant. It often surprises new creators. A good rule of thumb is that RPM runs at roughly 40% to 55% of CPM. That depends on your niche, your audience's location, and your content format.

Here is a concrete example with round numbers:

- Advertiser CPM: $8.00
- After YouTube's 45% cut: $4.40 per thousand ad impressions
- Assume 60% of total views are monetized impressions (a common mid-range estimate)
- Effective RPM from ads alone: $4.40 x 0.60 = **$2.64**

Add in YouTube Premium revenue, which [YouTube describes](https://support.google.com/youtube/answer/9314964) as distributed based on watch time from Premium members, and you might reach $3.00 to $3.50 RPM on that same channel.

[Influencer Marketing Hub's 2024 analysis](https://influencermarketinghub.com/youtube-money-calculator/) of YouTube earnings, which pulls together creator-reported data, puts average RPM across niches at roughly $1.50 to $4.00. Finance and business content can reach $6.00 to $10.00 RPM. Entertainment or gaming content often sits below $2.00. These are ranges, not guarantees. Your numbers depend on your audience's location, device type, and viewing behavior.

---

## Why does CPM vary so much by niche?

Advertisers bid on audiences, not content. A financial services advertiser will pay much more to reach someone researching retirement accounts than a snack brand will pay to reach someone watching gaming highlights.

[Statista's digital advertising data](https://www.statista.com/statistics/190711/estimated-advertising-revenue-of-google-since-2001/) shows Google's advertising revenue, which funds YouTube, is heavily weighted toward high-intent commercial categories. That flows downstream into CPM differences by niche.

Here is what that means in practice. A personal finance channel with 50,000 monthly views can out-earn a gaming channel with 500,000 monthly views on RPM alone. That happens purely because advertisers want that finance audience more.

## Which metric should you actually track week to week?

Track RPM as your main revenue health metric. It reflects actual dollars earned per thousand total views, not just monetized ones. CPM tells you what advertisers are paying. RPM tells you what you are keeping. A rising CPM with a flat RPM usually means more unmonetized traffic is eating into your effective earnings.

[Track RPM as your primary revenue health metric](/blog/how-to-increase-your-youtube-rpm/). Monitor CPM as a signal about audience value to advertisers.

If your CPM rises but your RPM stays flat, a larger share of your views are probably coming from unmonetized sources. That could be subscribers using ad blockers, traffic from regions with low ad fill rates, or a spike in views on older content that advertisers are not actively targeting.

If your RPM drops while CPM holds steady, check your traffic sources. A viral video that pulls in a flood of low-engagement views can pull your RPM down without touching your CPM at all.

YouTube Analytics lets you filter RPM by geography, traffic source, and content type. Using those filters is the only way to see which parts of your channel are actually making money and which parts are just adding views.

---

## Does RPM account for all my YouTube income?

Yes, if you use YouTube's built-in monetization tools. RPM in YouTube Studio adds together AdSense revenue, YouTube Premium revenue, channel memberships, Super Chats, and Super Thanks. It divides all of that by your total views.

It does not include money from outside sponsorships, affiliate links in your description, or Patreon revenue driven by your YouTube audience. Those income streams live outside YouTube's system. To see your full picture, you need to add those numbers on top of your RPM earnings yourself.

For creators building income from multiple sources, RPM is the floor, not the ceiling. But it is the most honest floor you have. It is the number worth understanding before you make any decisions about content strategy, upload frequency, or whether ad revenue alone can support your work.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the difference between YouTube RPM and CPM?
RPM (Revenue Per Mille) is the metric that actually predicts your payout because it reflects money you keep after YouTube's cut. CPM measures what advertisers pay per 1,000 ad impressions, while RPM measures what you earn per 1,000 total video views across all monetization sources. CPM is always higher than RPM. For most creators, RPM runs roughly 45-55% of CPM once YouTube's 45% revenue share and non-monetized views are factored in.

### Why is my YouTube RPM so much lower than my CPM?
Your RPM is lower because not every view serves an ad, and YouTube keeps 45% of ad revenue. CPM only counts views where ads actually ran, while RPM divides your earnings across all views, including those with no ads. A video with 100,000 views might only generate 60,000 monetized impressions, which shrinks the effective rate right away. Non-skippable ads, viewer location, and watch time all further influence the gap between the two numbers.

### What is a good YouTube RPM for creators in 2024?
A good YouTube RPM typically ranges from $2 to $10 for general lifestyle content, while finance, legal, and software niches regularly see $10 to $30 or higher. RPM varies a lot by audience geography, seasonality, and content category. Q4 consistently produces the highest RPMs because of advertiser holiday spending. Creators should compare their RPM to others in their specific niche rather than to platform-wide averages, which are pulled down by high-volume, low-RPM channels.

### Should I use CPM or RPM to estimate my YouTube earnings?
Always use RPM to estimate your actual YouTube earnings. CPM is an advertiser-facing metric that overstates creator income because it leaves out YouTube's revenue share and non-monetized views. Multiply your RPM by your projected total views (divided by 1,000) to get a realistic payout estimate. For example, a $5 RPM on 200,000 monthly views yields roughly $1,000 before taxes. That makes RPM the only number worth tracking for personal income forecasting.

### Does YouTube RPM include income from channel memberships and Super Chats?
Yes, YouTube RPM includes revenue from channel memberships, Super Chats, Super Stickers, and YouTube Premium watch time, not just ad revenue. This makes RPM a more complete picture of your earning rate than CPM alone. Because these sources are divided across all views, a channel with highly engaged fans who buy memberships will show a meaningfully higher RPM than a similar channel that relies only on ad income.
