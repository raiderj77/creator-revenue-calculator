# Podcast Revenue Calculator: 7 Mistakes That Wreck Your Income Estimates

Most podcasters overestimate their income by 3x to 10x before they launch. They use best-case CPM numbers, ignore platform fees, and forget that downloads and listeners are not the same thing. This post shows the real math, the real ranges, and the specific mistakes that turn a promising revenue plan into a disappointing spreadsheet.

---

## Are you confusing downloads with listeners?

Downloads and unique listeners are different numbers. Most ad networks pay on downloads, not listeners. But one listener can trigger multiple downloads if they use auto-download settings.

Here is why this matters for your calculator. If you have 1,000 downloads per episode, you might assume 1,000 people heard your ad. But Spotify for Podcasters and Apple Podcasts both report that a single subscriber can generate 2 to 4 downloads per month depending on their app settings. Your true audience reach may be half your download count or less.

Ad buyers know this. That is why many networks use a 30-day download window to count an episode's performance. Spotify's own publisher documentation confirms that "downloads" include any file request, including ones that never finished playing.

**What to do instead:** Use your "consumption rate" if your host reports it. Buzzsprout, Transistor, and Podbean all show what percentage of an episode gets played. A 60% completion rate on 1,000 downloads is closer to 600 real listeners. Build your revenue estimate on that number, not the raw download count.

---

## Are you using CPM numbers from success stories instead of network rate cards?

The $25 to $50 CPM figure gets repeated constantly in podcasting articles. It is real, but it is not the starting point. It is the ceiling for established shows in high-value niches.

Here is the actual range you should use in a calculator. Midroll, one of the larger podcast ad networks, has historically listed host-read mid-roll ads at $18 to $25 CPM for shows with 10,000 or more downloads per episode. Pre-roll rates run $15 to $18 CPM. Dynamically inserted ads from programmatic networks often pay $2 to $10 CPM, similar to display advertising.

If your show has under 5,000 downloads per episode, most major networks will not accept you. AdvertiseCast, which publishes a rate calculator on its website, shows average rates around $18 for mid-roll at the 10k threshold. Below that, you are negotiating direct deals or using host-read ads from smaller sponsors, often at flat fees rather than CPM.

**A concrete example:** 3,000 downloads per episode, one mid-roll ad, direct deal at $15 CPM. That is $45 per episode. Two episodes a week is $90 a week, or roughly $360 a month before taxes and before your hosting fees.

That is real money. But it is not the $2,000 a month number that circulates in podcasting communities.

---

## Are you ignoring the platform fee stack?

Every platform takes a cut. Most calculators ignore this or bury it. The fees add up fast, and they come from multiple directions at once.

Here is the stack for a typical independent podcaster:

- **Hosting fees:** Buzzsprout's paid plans start at $12 a month. Transistor starts at $19 a month. These are fixed costs that come out before any revenue.
- **Marketplace fees:** If you use Podcorn or a similar marketplace to find sponsors, they take a percentage of the deal. Podcorn lists its fee at 30% for marketplace-matched deals.
- **Payment processing:** If you use Stripe for listener support or membership payments, Stripe charges 2.9% plus $0.30 per transaction.
- **Patreon fees:** Patreon charges between 5% and 12% of your monthly earnings depending on your plan, plus payment processing fees. This is documented in Patreon's own pricing page.

**What this looks like in practice:** You land a $200 sponsorship deal through a marketplace. After a 30% marketplace fee, you have $140. After self-employment tax (roughly 15.3% in the US for sole proprietors), you net about $118. Your hosting costs $19 that month. You walk away with roughly $99 from a $200 deal.

A good podcast revenue calculator accounts for all of these layers. Most free ones online do not.

---

## Are you treating Patreon income as stable when it is not?

Patreon looks like recurring revenue. It is, but it churns. Most creators do not plan for churn in their income models.

Patreon has shared data in the past showing that average patron retention varies widely by niche and posting frequency. Industry observers who track creator economy data, including those at Creator IQ and Influencer Marketing Hub, consistently note that monthly churn rates for podcast Patreon pages tend to run between 5% and 15% per month for newer shows.

At 10% monthly churn, a page with 100 patrons at $5 each ($500 a month) loses 10 patrons in month one. If you add 8 new ones, you are at 98 patrons, not 108. After six months of this math, your income is lower than when you started, even with consistent new signups.

**What to do instead:** In your calculator, model three scenarios. Best case: churn is 3%, new signups outpace losses. Middle case: churn is 8%, income stays roughly flat. Worst case: churn is 15%, income declines without aggressive promotion. Base your spending decisions on the middle case, not the best case.

---

## Are you forgetting that most podcast income is episodic, not monthly?

Sponsorship income is not a salary. It is per episode, per campaign, or per quarter. Many podcasters budget as if it is monthly income when it is actually lumpy.

A sponsor might book a 6-episode run. You earn $600 over three weeks. Then there is a gap. Then another sponsor books 4 episodes. The total looks good on paper for the year. But in February and August, you might earn nothing from sponsorships.

This is not a flaw in podcasting as a business. It is just how the ad market works. Advertising spend clusters around Q4 (October through December) and drops in Q1 (January and February). The Interactive Advertising Bureau has documented this seasonal pattern in its annual podcast advertising revenue reports.

**What to do instead:** Calculate your annual sponsorship estimate first. Then divide by 12 to get a monthly average. Treat that average as a planning number, not a guaranteed monthly deposit. Keep 2 to 3 months of operating costs in reserve for the slow months.

---

## Are you overestimating how fast your audience will grow?

Most revenue calculators let you enter a growth rate. Most people enter an optimistic one. This single input can make a $500 a year projection look like $5,000 a year.

Here is a grounding fact. Edison Research's Infinite Dial report, which tracks US podcast listening habits annually, has consistently shown that podcast discovery is slow and mostly word-of-mouth. Most new shows plateau below 1,000 downloads per episode within their first year.

If you grow at 10% per month from 500 downloads per episode, you hit 1,500 downloads in about 11 months. That is solid growth. But at $18 CPM with one ad per episode and two episodes a week, you are earning about $54 a week at the end of that year. Not $540. Not $5,400.

**What to do instead:** Use a conservative growth rate of 5% to 8% per month for your base case. Run the numbers at 0% growth too. If the income is not worth it at flat growth, you need more revenue streams or a longer time horizon.

---

## Are you counting pre-launch listener promises as real revenue?

Some podcasters launch a Patreon or a paid community before their show goes live. They get signups from friends, colleagues, and early followers. They put those numbers into their revenue calculator and feel good.

Pre-launch signups are not the same as sustained revenue. Churn in the first 60 days after launch is often the highest it will ever be. People sign up out of curiosity or loyalty. They cancel when the show does not match what they expected, when episodes are inconsistent, or simply when the novelty fades.

There is no single published study that covers all podcast pre-launch churn, but the pattern is well-documented in creator economy commentary. Creators who have shared their Patreon data publicly on platforms like YouTube or in newsletters (Jack Conte, the co-founder of Patreon, has discussed creator economics in interviews) consistently note that the first 90 days are a filtering period, not a growth period.

**What to do instead:** Do not include pre-launch signups in your 12-month revenue model. Model them separately as a "launch bonus" that may or may not persist. Build your base revenue estimate on what you can earn with zero existing audience, then layer in growth from there.

---

## What does a realistic podcast revenue estimate actually look like?

Realistic year-one estimates include: 2 episodes/week, starting at 300 downloads growing to 800 by month 12, $15 CPM sponsorship, 50 Patreon patrons at $5 each with 8% churn, and hosting fees of $19 monthly.


Here is a simple, honest model for a show in year one.

**Assumptions:**
- 2 episodes per week
- Starting at 300 downloads per episode, growing to 800 by month 12
- One mid-roll sponsorship per episode at $15 CPM (direct deals, no network)
- 50 Patreon patrons at $5 each by month 6, with 8% monthly churn
- Podcorn marketplace for some deals (30% fee applied)
- Hosting at $19 a month

**Year one estimate:**
- Sponsorship revenue (gross): roughly $1,400 to $2,200 for the year
- After marketplace fees on half the deals: subtract $200 to $300
- Patreon gross (months 6 through 12): roughly $1,050 to $1,400
- After Patreon fees (8% plan): subtract $84 to $112
- Hosting costs: $228 for the year
- Net revenue estimate: $1,900 to $3,000 for the year, before income tax

That is $158 to $250 a month on average. It is not nothing. For a side project, it can cover your costs and then some. But it is not a living wage, and it is not the number most podcasting income articles lead with.

A good podcast revenue calculator shows you this range. It does not show you the ceiling and call it typical.

## Frequently asked questions

### Сколько денег зарабатывает подкаст с 10 000 слушателями?
Подкаст с 10 000 слушателями в среднем зарабатывает от $500 до $2 000 в месяц через спонсорство. Стандартная ставка CPM для подкастов составляет $18–$50 за тысячу прослушиваний, в зависимости от ниши и вовлечённости аудитории. Дополнительный доход можно получить через Patreon, Substack или прямые партнёрства. Реальные цифры сильно варьируются в зависимости от формата, частоты выпусков и лояльности слушателей.

### Как рассчитать доход от спонсорства подкаста?
Доход от спонсорства рассчитывается по формуле: CPM × (количество загрузок ÷ 1 000). Например, при 5 000 загрузок и CPM $25 один рекламный блок принесёт $125. Большинство подкастов размещают два-три рекламных блока за эпизод, pre-roll, mid-roll и post-roll. Mid-roll традиционно стоит дороже всего. Используйте калькулятор на сайте, чтобы смоделировать реалистичный доход с учётом вашей аудитории.

### Что такое CPM подкаста и какой показатель считается хорошим?
CPM подкаста, это стоимость тысячи прослушиваний, которую платит рекламодатель. Хорошим считается CPM от $20 до $40, а в нишах финансов, технологий или бизнеса он может достигать $50–$80. Начинающие подкасты с небольшой, но целевой аудиторией нередко получают более высокий CPM, чем массовые шоу. Вовлечённость и демография слушателей важнее, чем просто количество загрузок.

### Стоит ли запускать Patreon для подкаста с маленькой аудиторией?
Да, Patreon может быть прибыльным даже при небольшой аудитории, если у вас есть лояльные слушатели. Подкаст с 1 000 преданных слушателей и конверсией 5% при подписке $5/месяц приносит $250 ежемесячно. Это скромно, но стабильно. Ключ к успеху, предложить реальную ценность: ранний доступ, бонусные эпизоды или закрытое сообщество. Patreon лучше работает как дополнение к спонсорству, а не замена ему.

### Почему реальный доход подкастера отличается от расчётного?
Реальный доход часто ниже расчётного из-за нерегулярных спонсорских сделок, сезонности рекламного рынка и высоких комиссий платформ. Patreon берёт 5–12%, хостинги подкастов, свою долю, налоги съедают ещё часть. Кроме того, многие подкастеры переоценивают конверсию аудитории в платящих подписчиков. Калькулятор помогает увидеть реалистичный диапазон, а не идеальный сценарий, чтобы принимать взвешенные решения о монетизации.
