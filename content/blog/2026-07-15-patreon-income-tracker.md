# Patreon Income Tracker: 7 Mistakes That Wreck Your Numbers

Most Patreon income trackers fail for one reason: creators track the wrong number. They track gross pledges, not what actually lands in the bank. Patreon takes a flat 10% platform fee for new creators (joined on or after August 4, 2025), plus [payment processing fees and sometimes a 2.5% currency conversion fee](/blog/patreon-revenue/). Track net, not gross.

## Am I Tracking the Wrong Number From the Start?

Yes, if you're tracking total pledges instead of actual payout. Your Patreon dashboard shows pledge totals. That's not your income. Your real income is what hits your bank account after fees.

This mistake happens because [Patreon's main dashboard is built to show growth, not take-home pay](/blog/how-much-does-patreon-take/). It's motivating to watch pledges climb. But pledges are a promise, not a payment. Real income comes after three things get subtracted.

First, the platform fee. As of August 4, 2025, Patreon charges a flat 10% fee to all creators who join on or after that date. If you joined before that date, you may still be on legacy pricing: Lite (5%), Pro (8%), or Premium (12%), based on your old plan. Check your settings page to confirm which one applies to you. Don't assume.

Second, payment processing fees. These come on top of the platform fee. Patreon uses Stripe and PayPal depending on the payment method. These fees typically run in the range of 2.9% plus 30 cents per transaction, similar to standard card processing rates, though exact rates can vary by country and payment type. Check Patreon's help center for current rates before you build your model.

Third, currency conversion. If a patron pays in a currency different from your payout currency, Patreon applies a 2.5% conversion fee. If you have international patrons, this adds up fast.

**How to avoid it:** Build your tracker around net income, not gross pledges. A simple formula: Gross pledges minus platform fee minus processing fees minus conversion fees equals what you actually take home. Update this every payout cycle, not just once a year.

## Am I Forgetting That Patreon Pricing Isn't the Same for Everyone?

Yes, if you're using one fee number for your whole tracker without checking your join date. Patreon runs two different fee systems right now. Mixing them up will throw off every projection you make.

Here's why this trips people up. Patreon changed its fee structure on August 4, 2025. Anyone who joined the platform on or after that date pays a flat 10% platform fee. No tiers, no choices. Simple.

But creators who joined before that date may still be on the old system. That system had three tiers: Lite at 5%, Pro at 8%, and Premium at 12%. These older tiers are legacy pricing. They still apply to creators who set up their page before the change, but only if those creators haven't moved to the new structure.

This matters because a lot of income calculators and blog posts you'll find online were written before August 2025. They quote the old 5/8/12 tier system as if it's still the standard. It's not, for new creators. If you started your page after August 4, 2025, you are on the 10% flat rate. Full stop.

**How to avoid it:** Check your Patreon settings page to confirm your join date and your current fee tier. Label your tracker clearly. If you're on legacy pricing, write "legacy" next to your fee rate so future-you remembers why the math looks different from a friend's newer page.

## Am I Counting Pledges That Never Actually Get Charged?

Yes, if you're adding up pledge amounts without accounting for failed payments. Not every pledge turns into cash. Cards expire. Payments decline. Patrons cancel mid-month. Your tracker needs to account for this gap.

This mistake happens because the pledge number feels final. A patron signs up for $10 a month, so you count $10 a month, forever, in your head. But Patreon processes payments at the start of each month (for most creators using per-month billing), and not every charge goes through cleanly.

Declined payments happen for boring reasons: expired cards, insufficient funds, banks flagging the charge as suspicious. Patreon does retry failed payments, but retries don't always succeed. Over a year, even a small failure rate adds up. If 5% of your charges fail in any given month, and you have $2,000 in pledges, that's $100 you counted but never received.

**How to avoid it:** Track actual payouts, not pledge totals, every single month. Don't assume last month's pledge number equals this month's payout. Pull the real number from your Patreon payout history and log it directly. If you want to estimate future income, apply a small discount, maybe 3-5%, to account for typical payment failures. Adjust that number over time based on your own data.

## Am I Ignoring Patron Churn When I Project Future Income?

Yes, if your tracker assumes every current patron stays forever. Patrons cancel. Some cancel after one month. Some stick around for years. Averages hide a lot of movement underneath.

This mistake is common because churn feels invisible day to day. You gain three patrons, lose two, and the net number looks stable. But stable net numbers can hide high turnover. High turnover means your income is less predictable than it looks, even if the total stays flat.

There's no single universal churn rate creators can rely on because it varies wildly by niche, price point, and how often you post. A $3 tier with light engagement will churn differently than a $25 tier with monthly video calls. Don't import someone else's churn rate as your own. Build it from your data.

**How to avoid it:** Track new patrons and cancellations separately, every month, not just the net change. Over six months, you'll start to see your own churn pattern. Use that pattern, not a guess, to project three months out. If you consistently lose 8% of patrons a month, plan your income estimates around that, not around your best month ever.

## Am I Mixing Up Different Income Types on One Line?

Yes, if you lump Patreon income, sponsorship money, and merchandise sales into a single "creator income" number. They behave differently. Tracking them together hides which part of your business is actually working.

This happens because it's simpler to track one big number. But Patreon income, ad revenue, sponsorship deals, and one-time merch sales all have different growth patterns, different reliability, and different fee structures. Patreon takes its cut automatically every month. A sponsorship deal might pay once, 60 days after the video goes live. Merch sales might come through a third-party printer with its own fee schedule entirely separate from Patreon's.

If you blend these into one number, you can't tell whether your recurring income is actually growing or whether one big sponsorship check is masking a decline in patron support.

**How to avoid it:** Keep separate columns or separate tabs for each income source. Patreon net income should be its own line. Sponsorships, ad revenue, and merch should each get their own line too. Only combine them at the very end, for a total monthly view. This way you can see, at a glance, whether your Patreon page is actually growing or shrinking on its own.

## Am I Forgetting Taxes Until It's Too Late?

Yes, if your tracker shows Patreon payouts as pure profit. It's not. Depending on where you live, you likely owe income tax on that money, and in the US, self-employment tax too.

This mistake happens because Patreon doesn't take taxes out for you. The number that lands in your bank account looks final. It isn't. In the US, if you earn income as an independent creator, that income is generally subject to self-employment tax in addition to regular income tax. Patreon may issue a 1099 form if you cross certain earnings thresholds, but you owe tax on the income regardless of whether you receive a form. Rules vary by country, so check with a tax professional or your country's tax authority for exact requirements.

Many creators discover this the hard way, in April, when a tax bill shows up on income they already spent. That's a bad time to learn this lesson.

**How to avoid it:** Set aside a percentage of every Patreon payout the moment it arrives, before you spend any of it. Many creators use a range around 25-30% as a rough starting estimate for combined tax obligations, but your real number depends on your total income, deductions, and local tax rules. Talk to an accountant to get your actual rate. Keep that tax money in a separate savings account, untouched, until it's time to pay.

## Am I Comparing My Numbers to Someone Else's Highlight Reel?

Yes, if you're benchmarking your Patreon income against screenshots you saw online. Public income reports are almost always the best month, not the typical month. Comparing your average to someone else's peak will always make you feel behind.

This happens because creators who share income screenshots tend to share good news. Nobody posts a screenshot of a rough month with high churn and three declined payments. So the public picture of Patreon income skews high, even when it's not intended to mislead.

Real income data is harder to find than hype. Patreon has released some platform-wide figures in earnings reports and investor materials over the years, but these are company-wide totals, not typical per-creator numbers, and they don't tell you what a creator in your specific niche, with your specific audience size, should expect. Be skeptical of any number that doesn't name its source clearly.

**How to avoid it:** Track your own trend line over time instead of comparing to other creators. Look at your month-over-month growth rate, not your absolute dollar amount next to someone else's. If you want outside benchmarks, look for named, sourced reports, not screenshots, and read the methodology before you trust the number.

## Building a Tracker That Tells the Truth

A good Patreon income tracker isn't complicated. It just needs to track the right things: net payouts after fees, patron churn separate from new signups, taxes set aside before you spend, and each income source on its own line. Update it every payout cycle. The goal isn't a bigger number. The goal is an honest one, so you can make real decisions about your creator business instead of guessing.

## Frequently asked questions

### How much does Patreon take from creator earnings?
Patreon takes between 5% and 12% of your monthly income, depending on the plan tier you select (Lite, Pro, or Premium). On top of that, payment processing fees apply, typically 2.9% plus $0.30 per transaction, or higher for international patrons. So a creator earning $1,000/month might actually net $850–$900 after all fees. Always calculate your take-home pay using both Patreon's platform fee and processing costs, since the advertised percentage alone underestimates your true deductions.

### Is Patreon income considered taxable?
Yes, all Patreon income is taxable and must be reported as self-employment income in most countries. If you earn over $600 annually in the US, Patreon issues a 1099-K or 1099-NEC form, but you owe taxes even below that threshold. You'll also need to set aside money for self-employment tax (Social Security and Medicare), typically 15.3% in the US, plus federal and state income tax. Track expenses like equipment and software to reduce your taxable income.

### How many patrons do I need to replace a full-time salary?
It depends heavily on your average pledge amount and platform fees, but as a rough benchmark, replacing a $50,000/year salary requires roughly 400–800 patrons at $5–$10/month tiers. This assumes typical churn rates of 5–10% monthly, meaning you must continuously acquire new supporters. Creators with higher-value tiers ($20+) need fewer patrons but often have smaller audiences. Use an income calculator that factors in churn, fees, and tier distribution for a realistic projection.

### How does Patreon income compare to YouTube ad revenue?
Patreon income is generally more predictable but slower to scale than YouTube CPM earnings, which fluctuate with views, niche, and seasonality. YouTube CPMs typically range from $2–$10 per 1,000 views, meaning creators need massive view counts for meaningful income, while Patreon rewards deep audience loyalty over sheer reach. Many successful creators combine both: YouTube for discovery and top-of-funnel income, Patreon for stable recurring revenue from superfans. Diversifying across platforms reduces reliance on any single algorithm or fee structure.

### Why did my Patreon income drop even though my patron count stayed the same?
This usually happens due to payment failures, currency conversion losses, or patron downgrades between tiers. Roughly 10–15% of pledges fail each billing cycle due to expired cards or insufficient funds, and Patreon doesn't always retry immediately. Additionally, if you have international patrons, currency fluctuations can quietly reduce your payout even if pledge amounts look unchanged. Reviewing your monthly analytics dashboard for failed payments and tier shifts helps identify exactly where the discrepancy is occurring.
